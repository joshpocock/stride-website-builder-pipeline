# The Cinematic Frame Method

A six-layer prompt framework for generating hero images that actually look like they cost $10K to shoot. Use this as the thinking framework behind every start-frame / end-frame pair generated by `image-gen-nanobanana.md`. This is the "how to think about cinematic prompts" layer; the fill-in templates in `image-gen-nanobanana.md` are the "how to execute" layer.

**Provider-agnostic.** The framework works identically across Gemini 3 Pro Image (Wavespeed, 4K native, $0.025/img — the pipeline's preferred provider), Nano Banana Pro (Kie.ai or Wavespeed, 2K, $0.030/img), and Nano Banana / Gemini 2.5 Flash Image (any provider, 2K, $0.015–0.020/img for fast iteration). The prompt language does not change between providers; only the model name in the API call does. See `image-gen-nanobanana.md` for the provider selection table.

## The Core Principle

Most people write image prompts like grocery lists. They list things that should appear and hope the model figures out the rest. That is why 90 percent of AI images look generic.

A cinematic frame is not a list. It is a one-take film brief. You are not telling the model *what exists* in the frame, you are telling it *how light, material, and atmosphere behave together*. Write every prompt like you are briefing a cinematographer, not ordering a pizza.

**The weighting rule.** In any cinematic frame, lighting is roughly 60 percent of the final impression, the hero subject is 25 percent, and everything else (environment, atmosphere, camera) splits the remaining 15 percent. Spend your words accordingly. If your prompt has four sentences about the subject and one sentence about light, you have written the image backwards.

## The 6 Layers

### Layer 1 — ANCHOR

The single object the viewer's eye locks onto within the first half-second.

- **What it is.** One noun, not three. Pick the thing. (A brass chronograph watch. A carved obsidian monolith. A single ceramic espresso cup.)
- **What it is made of.** Name the exact material and one tactile detail. "Brushed titanium with machined radial grain" beats "metal."
- **How light behaves on it.** This is the upgrade most prompts skip. Glass refracts, steel has specular hotspots, matte ceramic swallows light, liquid has caustics, skin has subsurface scatter. Tell the model which physics to use.
- **Scale.** Relative to something knowable — a human hand, a doorway, a mountain, a grain of sand.

### Layer 2 — WORLD

Where the anchor lives and, more importantly, how the environment reacts to it.

- **Placement in frame.** Rule of thirds, dead center, looming overhead, buried in the lower third. Be specific. "Upper right quadrant, breaking the horizon line" beats "on the right."
- **Depth.** Is the anchor inches from the lens or a thousand feet away? Close enough that the viewer feels pressed against it, or distant enough that the environment is the real story?
- **Environment behavior.** Environments are never neutral — they react. Smoke coils around the anchor. Water tracks toward it. Sand streams past it. Clouds part above it. If your environment just sits there, the image feels dead.
- **Secondary anchors.** One to three supporting elements that give the frame weight — a distant ridge, a cracked wall, a single bird, a second object half-hidden in shadow.

### Layer 3 — LUMINANCE

The mood engine. Spend the most words here.

- **Source.** Where the light actually originates. Self-emissive (the anchor glows from within), single key (sun, window, practical), ambient (overcast, moonlight), rim (behind the subject), or multi-source (name each one and its job).
- **Quality.** Hard or soft. Volumetric god rays, razor-cut shadows, soft diffused wrap, harsh top-down, golden-hour rim. The one-word shortcut is never enough — describe what the light *does*, not what it is called.
- **Interaction.** How does the light hit specific surfaces? "The key light catches the top edge of the watch bezel and bounces warm amber into the brushed face, leaving the lower half in deep shadow." That sentence alone does more work than a full paragraph of generic mood words.
- **Palette.** Two dominant colors plus one accent. Name them specifically. "Deep navy, bone white, and a single stripe of burnt copper" beats "dark and warm."
- **Contrast.** High-contrast chiaroscuro, mid-contrast cinematic, or low-contrast dreamy wash.

### Layer 4 — AIR

What is between the camera and the subject. This layer is what separates amateur renders from cinematography.

- **Particulate.** Fog, mist, haze, dust motes, pollen, embers, snow, rain, falling ash. Name the density — thin drifting haze vs. thick volumetric fog.
- **Emotional register.** Two or three words. "Reverent, ancient, still." "Tense, kinetic, electric." "Quiet, intimate, lived-in." Pick words that could describe a film scene, not a stock photo.
- **Scale feeling.** How the frame should make the viewer feel in their body — overwhelmed, calmed, compressed, dwarfed, invited, shut out.

### Layer 5 — OPTICS

Camera and technical spec. Keep it tight — four to six terms is enough.

- **Shot type.** Cinematic wide, extreme wide, medium, macro, low-angle, overhead, Dutch tilt.
- **Focal length.** 14mm and 24mm for epic scale and edge distortion. 35mm and 50mm for natural human eye. 85mm for flattering compression. 100mm macro for product detail.
- **Film or sensor.** "Shot on 35mm Kodak Portra 400," "shot on ARRI Alexa LF," "Hasselblad medium format." Each has a distinct look the model has learned.
- **Depth of field.** Deep (everything sharp, epic feel), shallow (subject sharp, world melted into bokeh), or razor (only one plane in focus).
- **Render register.** Hyper-photorealistic, painterly-photorealistic, editorial photography, matte painting hybrid. Pick one and commit.
- **Aspect ratio.** 16:9 hero, 9:16 mobile hero, 1:1 product, 21:9 ultrawide cinematic, 4:3 editorial. Nano Banana Pro supports all of these — match to the end use before you generate.

### Layer 6 — EXCLUSIONS

This is the layer most prompt frameworks do not have, and it is the single biggest quality jump. Nano Banana Pro respects explicit negative instructions. Use them.

For every frame, list what must NOT appear in the image. Common exclusions for cinematic product and hero work:

- No hands, no fingers, no partial bodies unless specifically requested
- No text, no logos, no watermarks, no captions
- No reflections of the camera, no lens flare unless called for
- No oversaturated cartoon colors, no neon, no HDR halos
- No cluttered background, no random props, nothing touching the frame edges
- No duplicate subjects, no mirrored artifacts, no extra limbs on any figure

Write these as a short clause at the end of the prompt: "No hands, no text, no lens flare, no edge clutter, clean negative space on all sides."

## The Assembly Formula

Combine the six layers into a single flowing paragraph. Do not use lists, tags, or bracketed sections. Write it the way a director would brief a crew at 5 a.m. on set.

> [Shot type + lens] of [anchor with material and one tactile detail], positioned [placement and depth], lit by [source and quality, with one specific interaction sentence]. [World — environment and how it behaves around the anchor], with [one or two secondary anchors]. [Air — particulate, mood words, scale feeling]. [Palette — two dominants plus one accent], [contrast level]. Shot on [film or sensor], [render register], [aspect ratio]. [Exclusions sentence listing negatives].

One paragraph. No bullet points. No headers. No asterisks. Just prose.

## Worked Example — Website Hero

**Input idea.** "A product hero shot for a premium titanium watch brand, dark moody aesthetic, should work as the opening frame of a scroll animation."

**Assembled prompt.**

> A cinematic extreme close-up shot on an 85mm lens of a single brushed titanium chronograph watch with machined radial grain on the face and a deep sapphire crystal, positioned dead center of the frame on an invisible surface, the lens almost kissing the bezel. A single hard key light rakes in from camera right at a low angle, catching the top edge of the bezel in a razor-thin rim of warm white light and bouncing a soft amber reflection into the brushed face, leaving the lower half of the watch in rich controlled shadow. The background is a seamless void of deep charcoal that fades to pure black at the edges, with a faint cool blue ambient fill that just barely defines the far shoulder of the case. Thin drifting particulate hangs in the light path — not fog, just enough dust to reveal the volumetric shape of the key light. The mood is precise, confident, and quietly expensive. Palette is matte charcoal, brushed titanium silver, and a single stripe of burnt amber where the key light hits the bezel. High contrast cinematic. Shot on ARRI Alexa LF, hyper-photorealistic editorial product photography, 16:9 widescreen. No hands, no text, no logos, no lens flare, no reflections of the camera, clean negative space on all sides, nothing touching the frame edges.

## Locked Pair Workflow — For Scroll Animation Heroes

This is the workflow that actually matters for website scroll animations. Every premium scroll-animation hero needs **two frames**, not one: a start frame and an end frame. Kling 3.0 (or any image-to-video model) then interpolates between them.

If you generate the two frames independently, they will not match. Different light, different camera, different palette, and the animation feels stitched together. That is the number one reason AI scroll animations look cheap.

**The fix: lock everything except the anchor state.**

1. Write one base prompt using the six layers above.
2. Generate the start frame.
3. For the end frame, **change only the ANCHOR layer — the subject state.** Keep WORLD, LUMINANCE, AIR, OPTICS, and EXCLUSIONS word-for-word identical. Only describe what the anchor is now doing (assembled → exploded, closed → open, whole → fragmented, still → mid-motion).
4. Feed both frames into Nano Banana Pro Edit (which accepts multi-image input) and ask it to match lighting and color across the pair. This is the edit-don't-reroll pass.
5. Hand the locked pair to Kling with a detailed motion prompt.

**Example locked pair for the watch above.**

- **Start frame.** *...a single brushed titanium chronograph watch, fully assembled and still, sitting dead center on an invisible surface...* (rest of prompt unchanged)
- **End frame.** *...a single brushed titanium chronograph watch, exploded into its component layers — bezel, crystal, dial, hands, movement, and case back — drifting apart in a vertical stack as if time itself had stopped pulling them together, each layer separated by roughly one inch of empty space, still lit identically, still dead center...* (rest of prompt unchanged word-for-word)

The locked-pair trick is the single biggest upgrade for scroll-animation hero images.

## Five Laws for Nano Banana Pro

1. **Edit, do not re-roll.** If the frame is 80 percent right, describe the specific fix in plain language. "Move the watch to the upper third. Make the rim light warmer. Thicken the dust in the foreground." Nano Banana Pro is unusually good at targeted edits — use it like a retoucher, not a slot machine.
2. **Describe light as behavior, not adjective.** "Warm light" is dead words. "Warm light catches the top edge of the bezel and spills into the brushed face" is a direction.
3. **Make the environment do something.** If your environment is static, your image is a flat card. Smoke coils, water tracks, clouds part, sand streams, shadows lean. Give the world a verb.
4. **Use scale anchors ruthlessly.** A hand, a coin, a doorway, a mountain, a single bird. Without an anchor, the viewer has no way to measure the frame, and the image feels dollhouse-fake.
5. **Name the mood out loud.** The model responds to emotional direction more than any other single instruction. "Quietly expensive." "Reverent." "Tense and electric." Pick two or three words and put them in the prompt.

## How This File Relates to `image-gen-nanobanana.md`

- `cinematic-frame-method.md` (this file) — the **thinking framework**. Use when you need to design a new prompt from scratch, deviate from the standard product-hero template, or produce something more atmospheric than a clean studio shot.
- `image-gen-nanobanana.md` — the **fill-in-the-blank templates** for the standard start-frame / end-frame product workflow. Use when the project is a normal product hero and the variables (product, background, vibe, brand colors) are already known.

Default pipeline behavior: use `image-gen-nanobanana.md` for speed. Fall back to this file when the project is non-standard (environmental heroes, abstract heroes, editorial/lifestyle scenes, luxury brand tone) or when the first pass from the templates does not land.
